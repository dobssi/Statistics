2. Capital gains tax analysis

2.1 Why Sweden dominates

As Swedish tax residents, Paul and Camilla are subject to Swedish CGT on worldwide disposals. The UK also taxes the gain but the UK–Sweden Double Tax Treaty ensures Sweden credits UK tax paid. Total tax is capped by Sweden — the higher-tax jurisdiction.

Sweden charges ~27% effective CGT (näringsbostad), does not allow April 2015 rebasing, provides no private residence relief, and critically taxes FX movements as real gains. FX is the single largest driver of the tax bill.

2.2 Swedish CGT methodology

Each component (purchase, improvements, sale) is converted into SEK at the Riksbank spot rate on the date it arose. The gain is calculated entirely in SEK. The taxable event is the binding contract date (exchange), not completion.

4. Rental income tax position

4.1 UK rental tax

Non-resident landlords under NRLS. Mortgage interest is a 20% credit (not deduction) since April 2020. SW3 mortgage not deductible. At Paul's 45% rate, UK rental tax on ~£160k net income ≈ £65–70k/year.

4.2 Swedish rental tax

Taxed as business income (inkomst av näringsverksamhet) at marginal rates up to ~50–55%. However, Sweden allows full mortgage interest deduction, building depreciation (2–3% p.a.), and all operating costs. Depreciation may be recaptured on sale.

10.2 The tenant trap

You cannot market a house for sale with tenants in it. With a 12-month break clause exercised in spring 2027, the property is vacant from ~May 2027 — exactly when the rate shock hits. Marketing empty on the variable rate costs ~£17–18k/month. The family has already endured 10 months of empty marketing at the lower fixed rate.

11. Key risks of holding

The empty-property trap is the most dangerous practical risk. Any hold strategy involving a future vacant marketing period risks a repeat of the 10-month experience — but at triple the mortgage cost after May 2027.

The mortgage rate shock on 31 May 2027 transforms hold economics from breakeven to ~£45k/year cash-negative.

FX drift increases Swedish CGT every year as GBP/SEK diverges from 2010–12 rates.

Market risk in prime London above £5m: stamp duty headwinds, reduced buyer pool, no obvious recovery catalyst.

Cross-border complexity: annual dual-jurisdiction rental tax filings, mortgage interest interaction, depreciation recapture.

12. CGT cash management on sale

Swedish CGT from a 2026 sale is reported in the spring 2027 return, with payment due ~autumn 2027 (18–20 months float). Skatteverket charges interest from 13 February 2027. Optimal strategy: convert estimated CGT to SEK after exchange, hold in high-quality savings until Skatteverket rate exceeds net-of-tax savings rate, then pay.

14. Conclusion & recommendation

The dual-marketing strategy has given the family two viable options. But the practical constraint of the tenant/vacancy cycle, combined with the May 2027 mortgage cliff, significantly weakens the hold case compared to how it appears on a spreadsheet.

At £5.5m or above, selling is the clear winner. No further analysis needed.

At £5.4m, selling is the financially safer choice. It banks ~£2.6m net after both mortgages, ERC, and all taxes. It eliminates the rate shock, empty-property trap, cross-border complexity, and FX drift. The £41.5k ERC is an irritation, not a deal-breaker.

The rental alternative at £20k/month is viable in the short term but carries a trap. The property covers costs for 14 months while the fixed rate lasts. But there is no clean path from "renting comfortably" to "sold at a good price" without passing through an expensive vacant marketing period on the variable rate — a situation the family has already endured for 10 months.

If the family does choose to rent, the honest commitment is a long hold (2–3 years), not a short bridge to spring 2027. A long hold requires accepting the variable rate, mitigating via remortgage or Värmdö paydown, and sustained cross-border complexity. This is defensible with genuine conviction about recovery — but it is a bet, not a plan.

What should be avoided at all costs is the middle path: a short let followed by another expensive empty marketing period, this time at triple the mortgage rate.
